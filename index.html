Describe risks that may impact digital projects

There are many risks that may be encountered on projects whether digital or not, and it is rare projects will go through to completion under ideal circumstances. For digital projects we can see some specific issues that may arise due to the amount of technology and costs involved.

Technology risks:
  • Hardware - Costly for initial layout and continuing maintenance. Is the team proficient in this using the hardware provided?
  • Software - Conflicting versions of OS if new ones are released during the project. Programmes may not be sufficient for your needs. Are your teams able to use the programmes you need them to in order to complete their tasks efficiently?
  • Skills - Are there any training requirements for any area of the project?
  • People issues - Depending on the scale of the project there may be a large amount of people in the team. This may lead to communication, personality, and consistency issues. Team members may leave or join the project throwing the skillset and dynamics out of sync.
  • Budgeting/Costs - Cost of digital projects are generally high due to sector trends and expectations of businesses and consumers to have the latest tech. Keeping within a budget may be difficult if problems arise with upgrading hardware/software or there are unavoidable external factors, e.g. flooding in the building that houses the project.
  • Competition - Competitors may release a similar website before you, putting your project into uncertainty, and could also draw away potential customers.
  • Planning - Risks may not have been sufficiently thought out during the planning process giving a 'best case scenario'. This can lead to unexpected issues that take valuable resource and cost away from the project and affect the timeline.

All of these factors and many more can lead to the failure of a digital project. The main theme to take away from this is plan plan plan! Then plan some more!

Legal guidelines and restraints that impact digital projects e.g. GDPR

GDPR allows users more control over how much of their personal data is stored and used by companies, giving them access to view, restrict or even delete the information held. This was brought about by the advancements of technology and global business giving rise to increased availability and use of personal data. Essentially they cannot hold data that they "may need for future". If companies do not adhere to these guidelines they can get sanctioned.

For digital projects we need to take this into account, to minimise the amount of personal data collected by limiting the scope for collecting this in the first place. This will also mean amending the website to ask for consent to use someones data. To this end, developers and designers on a project will need to understand basic GDPR and know what elements should be implemented to be compliant.

Access issues - issues disabled people may face when using webpages, how to overcome them

It is estimated that around 20% of people in the UK find it difficult to use technology because of a disability. As technology has advanced rapidly within the last few years, there is now a number of adaptations that they can take advantage of to get close and even full functionality of a website. There are a number of charities like Able Gamers, to name just one, that use a combination of technologies, e.g. mouth controllers and eye gaze (not an exhaustive list), that help people play computer games whatever their disability. We will look at the main disabilities and how technology can be adapted for them.

  • Blindness - Text-to-Braille or Text-to-Speech software. HTML can provide the text equivalent to visual information.
  • Poor eyesight/Colour blindness - Text and images can be enlarged, screen enlargers can be added externally. For colour blindness, links can be underlined/enboldened/colourised/patterned simultaneously for clarity even when the material is shown in colours. Using an "alt" text in HTML to describe an image and dark text over light backgrounds can help also.
  • Mobility disabilities - Navigation is a difficulty for people with loss of motor skills, tremors, lowered use of hands etc. Coding in a specific way can allow access and navigation via keyboard only or "single switch device" (e.g. joystick).
  • Hearing impairment/deafness - Although the web is generally a visual media, there is information to be gained via audio formats. The use of closed captions or sign language can help people with hearing difficulties or deafness to receive the same information as people with no hearing impairmemts.
  • Photosensitive seizures/Epilepsy - Seizures can be triggered by flashing effects. Although they improve the visual appeal of the website, the option to switch them off and a disclaimer for the flashing images must be added to the website.
  • Cognitive/Intellectual disabilities - e.g. dementia, dyslexia. Due to the complexity of website design or its content, people with cognitive disablities often find it difficult to understand the information provided. As well as making the content simple and easy to use, being consistent in colour, text font, navigation and colour contrast can be valid solutions for cognitive impairment.

Improving accessibility to technology ensures that your website is open to everyone. As well as expanding the potential audience, it can improve the overall design and usability for all users, making it more flexible and futureproof.

Appropriate filetypes for websites

When viewing websites there are a number of file types that will be encountered, some more frequently than others. In this section we will look at the most common.

  • .JPG/.JPEG - Common image format mainly used for photos.
  • .GIF - Most common. Can be animated. Can be interlaced, saves the graphic so it loads progressively.
  • .PNG - Designed for use in place of GIF's. Better version of GIF, except for animation.
  • .AVI - Standard video format. Cannot stream so file needs to be downloaded to view.
  • .MPEG/.MPG - Streams movies.
  • .MOV/.QT - QuickTime format for streaming movies.
  • .DOC - Microsoft Word Document. Most popular word proccesing tool.
  • .PDF - Adobe Acrobat files. Allows documents to be transferred between computers and platforms without changing the format or layout.
  • .TXT - Most basic of file types, i.e. text files from Notepad, Notes etc.
  • .EXE - Executable file. Format of programmes that require installation.
  • .MIDI - Music files normally made on keyboards. Very small in size and have good sound.
  • .MP3 - Compressed files making them easily downloaded. Good quality sound.
  • .WAV - Basic sound file for short samples.
  • .CSS - Used to make website look good. Describes the style of HTML and how it should be displayed. Can be initially written and edited in a text file then transferred. CSS can save a lot of work and can control the layout of multiple webpages at once.
  • .HTML - Creates the structure of webpages. Uses elements and tags to format the HTML content.
  • .ZIP - Different types of files that can compressed and archived. A program must be used to 'unzip' them, e.g. WinZip.
  • .RAR - Similar to .ZIP files but supports multimedia compression. WinRAR can be used to open the files.
As you can see there are a lot of different types of files to make your website as interesting and attractive as you want. Ultimately it will be dependant on what your website theme is and who your target audience is.

How to test digital solutions

Does my website work?

Yes! I have to tested it on different web browsers, for this project Chrome and Safari. In a wider project cross browser testing is used to test across multiple browsers. This checks for compatibility on different web browsers, e.g. Edge, Safari, Chrome.

There are a number of digital testing methods employed by developers to ensure that their software application works as intended upon release.

  1. Unit testing:
    Unit testing allows individual units of software to be tested. A unit is the smallest component that can be tested. Within programming, a unit could be a programme, function, procedure etc. The aim of unit testing is to isolate each component of the subject code, (e.g. programme, website etc) from the rest of the code and determine if it works as expected. This is usually done during development of the software application by the developers.

  2. Integration Testing:
    This checks if all components of the unit testing are working together. After the units are combined again, it will expose any faults when the units interact with each other and are tested as a group.

  3. System testing:
    Completed when the complete integrated software is to be tested as a whole. It evaluates the systems compliance with the requirements specified.

  4. User testing:
    Also known as UAT (User Acceptance Testing), is tested in a real world, typically by end users (clients) that would be using the application.
